# EARLY WARNING INDICATORS — TENSION ESCALATION

## Linked topic

`TOP-BIH-TENSION-ESCALATION`

## Topic name

Динамика и признаци за нарастване на политическото, институционалното и общественото напрежение в Босна и Херцеговина

---

# Purpose

This file defines the early-warning and escalation-indicator framework for:

`TOP-BIH-TENSION-ESCALATION`

Its purpose is to help the BiH Daily Topic Monitor distinguish between:

- routine political conflict;
- persistent structural tension;
- limited escalation;
- broad escalation;
- acute crisis.

The monitor must not assume that Bosnia and Herzegovina is escalating merely because:

- political rhetoric is hostile;
- institutions disagree;
- nationalist narratives are present;
- media coverage increases;
- several controversial events occur on the same day.

Escalation must be demonstrated through observable change.

---

# Exact monitored variable

The monitored variable is:

**Change in the intensity, scope, operational expression, institutional consequence, geographic spread, and security relevance of political, institutional, social, or inter-community confrontation in Bosnia and Herzegovina.**

The monitor is not measuring:

- general political dissatisfaction;
- permanent constitutional disagreement;
- ideological polarization alone;
- ordinary election competition;
- media attention.

The central question is:

**Has the level of confrontation materially changed compared with the previous known condition?**

---

# Core analytical distinction

Always distinguish:

1. CURRENT CONDITION
2. DAILY MOVEMENT
3. BROADER TRAJECTORY
4. ESCALATION POTENTIAL

These are not interchangeable.

A highly tense environment may remain:

`STABLE TENSION`

A relatively calm environment may show:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`

if a new mechanism of confrontation appears.

A serious political event does not automatically prove:

`ESCALATION`

---

# Baseline / normal tension condition

The following may be part of the persistent political environment in BiH and must not automatically be treated as escalation:

- harsh rhetoric between political blocs;
- criticism of OHR;
- criticism of the Constitutional Court;
- repeated secessionist rhetoric without new implementation;
- accusations of constitutional violations;
- routine veto use;
- ordinary parliamentary obstruction;
- repeated calls for institutional reform;
- political disputes between state and entity levels;
- election-related accusations;
- nationalist symbolic politics;
- repeated references to historical grievances;
- isolated inflammatory statements;
- routine opposition-government conflict;
- peaceful protests without escalation indicators;
- standard police presence at public events;
- routine international diplomatic concern.

These may still be analytically relevant.

However, they require additional evidence before they can support an escalation judgment.

---

# Indicator interpretation rule

Indicators are classified as:

- LOW-LEVEL
- MEDIUM-LEVEL
- HIGH-LEVEL
- CRITICAL

This classification reflects potential warning value.

It does not automatically determine:

- DAILY ASSESSMENT;
- SIGNIFICANCE;
- OVERALL DAILY ASSESSMENT.

One HIGH-level indicator may be less important than several mutually reinforcing MEDIUM-level indicators.

One indicator must never be interpreted mechanically.

Always assess:

- verification;
- duration;
- repetition;
- actor capability;
- institutional consequence;
- geographic spread;
- cross-topic linkage.

---

# LOW-LEVEL INDICATORS

Low-level indicators suggest increased pressure but do not by themselves establish escalation.

Examples include:

- measurably sharper rhetoric by major political actors;
- introduction of new hostile framing;
- increased use of existential or zero-sum narratives;
- new accusations of treason or illegitimacy;
- calls for political exclusion;
- repeated references to conflict-era symbols;
- increased symbolic confrontation;
- public disputes around flags, memorials, anniversaries, or identity symbols;
- new political ultimatums without implementation;
- announcement of possible institutional retaliation;
- intensified parliamentary confrontation;
- increased frequency of emergency political meetings;
- renewed discussion of withdrawal from institutions without concrete steps;
- isolated calls for protest;
- isolated hostile demonstrations;
- unusual but non-violent security rhetoric;
- increased diplomatic concern without operational measures.

## Interpretation

LOW-level indicators may justify:

- increased monitoring;
- broader search;
- watchlist inclusion.

They should not independently justify:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`

unless they form a sustained and corroborated pattern.

---

# MEDIUM-LEVEL INDICATORS

Medium-level indicators demonstrate movement beyond routine rhetoric or ordinary political disagreement.

Examples include:

- formal adoption of a contested institutional measure;
- binding decision that materially changes an existing dispute;
- refusal to implement a specific lawful decision;
- active obstruction of a key state function;
- repeated institutional non-compliance;
- initiation of procedures to neutralize another institution;
- creation of new parallel administrative mechanisms;
- formal withdrawal steps from a joint institution;
- serious breakdown of inter-institutional coordination;
- emergency parliamentary sessions linked to confrontation;
- coordinated political actions across several institutions;
- official criminal complaints emerging from high-level political confrontation;
- credible claims of physical intimidation involving political actors;
- organized protests with confrontational potential;
- significant increase in police deployment linked to political tension;
- new restrictions affecting a politically or ethnically sensitive symbol;
- coordinated nationalist mobilization;
- new institutional action around a sensitive anniversary;
- credible official warning that tensions are worsening;
- entry of a new capable actor into an existing confrontation.

## Interpretation

A single MEDIUM-level indicator may support:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`

only when:

- it represents genuine movement from the previous condition;
- the changed risk mechanism is explicit;
- the development is sufficiently verified.

Several mutually reinforcing MEDIUM indicators may represent an emerging escalation pattern.

---

# HIGH-LEVEL INDICATORS

High-level indicators demonstrate substantial deterioration in the risk environment.

Examples include:

- confirmed withdrawal from key state institutions;
- systematic refusal to implement binding state-level decisions;
- establishment of functioning parallel institutions;
- verified mass political mobilization with confrontation risk;
- coordinated mobilization across multiple locations;
- serious clashes between protesters and police;
- repeated politically or ethnically linked violent incidents;
- official activation of extraordinary security measures;
- credible security warnings from competent institutions;
- verified armed presence connected to political confrontation;
- severe obstruction of central state functionality;
- direct confrontation between entity and state security structures;
- credible threats of violence from actors with organizational capability;
- material geographic spread of confrontation;
- sustained movement from rhetoric to implementation;
- clear cross-border support for escalation;
- synchronized activity across political, organizational, and information domains;
- evidence that previously separate tensions are converging.

## Interpretation

HIGH-level indicators may justify:

`ESCALATING`

or an overall:

`ESCALATION`

assessment when the development is:

- verified;
- materially new;
- consequential;
- connected to a clear worsening mechanism.

Do not use HIGH merely because the topic is politically sensitive.

---

# CRITICAL INDICATORS

Critical indicators represent acute or immediate risk.

Examples include:

- confirmed armed confrontation;
- large-scale politically or ethnically motivated violence;
- credible imminent threat of organized violence;
- coordinated deployment of armed groups;
- major violent mobilization;
- breakdown of police or security command structures;
- direct confrontation between formal security bodies;
- widespread attacks on institutions;
- sustained multi-location violent unrest;
- credible operational preparation for violent action;
- major constitutional breakdown with immediate security consequences;
- mass casualty incident linked to political or inter-community confrontation;
- rapid cross-border transfer of violent actors or weapons;
- emergency international security posture explicitly linked to imminent risk.

## Interpretation

CRITICAL indicators require:

- very strong evidence;
- careful source verification;
- immediate differentiation between allegation and confirmed fact.

CRITICAL must remain exceptional.

---

# Escalation mechanisms

The monitor must identify the exact mechanism of escalation.

Use the form:

PREVIOUS CONDITION
→ NEW DEVELOPMENT
→ CHANGED RISK

Examples:

## Mechanism 1 — Rhetoric to formal action

Known political dispute
→ binding contested measure adopted
→ increased probability of institutional confrontation

## Mechanism 2 — Institutional dispute to non-compliance

Known disagreement
→ formal refusal to implement decision
→ reduced institutional functionality and higher confrontation risk

## Mechanism 3 — Political conflict to mobilization

Hostile rhetoric
→ organized calls for mass participation
→ increased probability of street-level confrontation

## Mechanism 4 — Localized tension to geographic spread

One local dispute
→ similar developments in multiple locations
→ broader destabilization risk

## Mechanism 5 — Symbolic conflict to security incident

Contested symbolic issue
→ organized provocation or violent incident
→ increased inter-community risk

## Mechanism 6 — Online hostility to offline action

Escalatory narratives
→ coordinated offline gathering or mobilization
→ higher real-world security risk

## Mechanism 7 — Institutional conflict to parallel structures

Political disagreement
→ establishment of alternative institutions or enforcement mechanisms
→ material constitutional and security risk

## Mechanism 8 — Domestic tension to cross-border reinforcement

Internal dispute
→ external actor, network, funding, or mobilization support
→ increased capability and persistence of confrontation

If no explicit mechanism can be identified, do not force an escalation assessment.

---

# Escalation sequence indicators

The monitor should actively test whether developments form a sequence.

Potential sequences include:

Rhetoric
→ formal decision
→ implementation
→ resistance
→ enforcement
→ confrontation

or:

Narrative escalation
→ mobilization call
→ organized gathering
→ provocation
→ incident
→ retaliation

or:

Institutional dispute
→ obstruction
→ refusal
→ parallel action
→ competing authority
→ security consequences

or:

Local incident
→ political amplification
→ copycat event
→ geographic spread
→ broader mobilization

A sequence is analytically stronger than several unrelated tense developments.

# Primary evidence requirement

Assessment must be driven mainly by:

`PRIMARY ASSESSMENT EVIDENCE`

Examples:

- implemented action;
- binding decision;
- verified incident;
- confirmed mobilization;
- operational security measure;
- documented geographic expansion;
- confirmed institutional breakdown.

The following normally remain:

`SUPPORTING CONTEXT`

unless they materially change the situation:

- repeated rhetoric;
- anniversary proximity;
- publication of already known legal reasoning;
- repeated commentary;
- continuation of an old dispute;
- increased article volume;
- symbolic reactions without implementation.

---

# False positives

The monitor must actively guard against false escalation signals.

## False positive 1 — Media volume

Many articles
→ not necessarily many developments

Twenty articles about one dispute remain one underlying development.

---

## False positive 2 — Election rhetoric

Campaign hostility
→ not automatically escalation

Look for:

- mobilization;
- coercion;
- violence;
- institutional consequences;
- security measures.

---

## False positive 3 — Anniversary effects

Sensitive anniversary
→ not automatically increased risk

Look for:

- actual mobilization;
- incidents;
- threats;
- security posture change.

---

## False positive 4 — Routine veto or obstruction

Use of existing constitutional mechanism
→ not automatically escalation

Assess whether:

- frequency changes;
- scope expands;
- state functionality deteriorates materially.

---

## False positive 5 — Strong statement by one actor

Extreme rhetoric
→ not automatically operational escalation

Assess:

- capability;
- follow-through;
- coordination;
- audience mobilization.

---

## False positive 6 — Several unrelated disputes

Temporal overlap
→ not automatically cross-topic pattern

Look for:

- common actors;
- shared mechanism;
- coordination;
- mutual reinforcement.

---

## False positive 7 — International concern

Diplomatic concern
→ not automatically evidence of worsening conditions

Assess whether international actors changed:

- force posture;
- security measures;
- policy;
- operational presence.

---

# De-escalation indicators

The monitor must also search for evidence of genuine reduction in tension.

Possible de-escalation indicators include:

- withdrawal of contested measure;
- suspension of implementation;
- restoration of institutional participation;
- negotiated compromise;
- resumption of blocked decision-making;
- reduction in police deployment;
- cancellation of provocative mobilization;
- public de-escalatory messaging followed by action;
- successful mediation;
- implementation of court or institutional decisions;
- reduction in incident frequency;
- absence of follow-on incidents after a previously rising cluster;
- coordinated calls for restraint by relevant actors;
- restoration of communication between institutions.

A quiet day alone is not de-escalation.

De-escalation requires evidence of reduced pressure or restored functionality.

---

# Stabilization indicators

Possible stabilization indicators include:

- dispute remains unresolved but does not worsen;
- planned escalation step is postponed;
- institutions continue functioning despite tension;
- no follow-on mobilization;
- no spread beyond original location;
- security institutions report no elevated threat;
- contested action remains procedural rather than operational;
- rhetoric continues without implementation.

These may support:

`STABLE TENSION`

# Evidence requirements

For LOW-level indicators:

- one credible source may be sufficient for discovery;
- additional verification is preferred.

For MEDIUM-level indicators:

- seek primary or official source;
- or credible independent corroboration.

For HIGH-level indicators:

- seek strong official evidence;
- or multiple genuinely independent credible sources;
- perform contradiction search.

For CRITICAL indicators:

- require the highest available verification standard;
- actively search for denial, correction, location mismatch, old footage, or source-chain dependence.

---

# Source-chain discipline

Do not treat:

multiple articles

as:

multiple independent confirmations

Ask:

- Are outlets copying the same agency?
- Are they quoting one politician?
- Are they reproducing one press release?
- Are they citing one anonymous source?

When independence is unclear use:

`reported by several outlets`

not:

`independently confirmed`

---

# Daily assessment decision rules

## STABLE

Use when:

- tension remains present;
- no meaningful worsening mechanism appears;
- primary evidence does not demonstrate change.

---

## LIMITED ESCALATION

Use when:

- at least one verified development materially increases risk;
- there is a clear escalation mechanism;
- evidence remains limited in scope;
- no broad mobilization or widespread deterioration is demonstrated.

Before assigning, state internally:

PREVIOUS CONDITION
→ NEW DEVELOPMENT
→ CHANGED RISK

---

## ESCALATING

Use when:

- several primary indicators demonstrate worsening;
- severity, scope, frequency, coordination, or geographic spread increases;
- movement beyond isolated institutional conflict is visible.

---

## MIXED

Use only when:

- genuine worsening occurs in one dimension;
- genuine stabilization or improvement occurs in another.

Do not use MIXED merely because positive and negative facts coexist.

---

## INSUFFICIENT DATA

Use when:

- comparison baseline is weak;
- source quality is insufficient;
- apparent escalation depends on unconfirmed signals;
- current movement cannot be separated from normal volatility.

---

# Significance discipline

Do not confuse:

`SIGNIFICANCE`

with:

`ESCALATION`

A development can be:

HIGH significance
+
STABLE assessment

Example:

A major court ruling may be institutionally important without increasing daily confrontation.

A development can also be:

MEDIUM significance
+
LIMITED ESCALATION

Example:

A localized but verified movement from rhetoric to confrontation.

---

# Watchlist requirements

Watch indicators must be:

- observable;
- procedurally possible;
- temporally plausible;
- linked to a specific escalation mechanism.

Good examples:

- whether a contested law completes the remaining legislative procedure;
- whether a competent court accepts a constitutional challenge;
- whether police formally respond to reported threats;
- whether planned protests expand geographically;
- whether political rhetoric is followed by institutional non-compliance;
- whether additional municipalities report related incidents.

Bad examples:

- “monitor the situation”;
- “watch for escalation”;
- “see whether tensions rise”;
- application of a law that has not yet entered into force.

---

# Final topic audit

Before assigning the daily assessment for:

`TOP-BIH-TENSION-ESCALATION`

verify:

1. What exactly changed today?
2. What is the previous comparison condition?
3. Which items are PRIMARY ASSESSMENT EVIDENCE?
4. Which items are only SUPPORTING CONTEXT?
5. Which items are BACKGROUND?
6. Are any serious claims still UNCONFIRMED SIGNALS?
7. Is the escalation mechanism explicit?
8. Would the assessment remain justified if context were removed?
9. Would the assessment remain justified if unconfirmed signals were removed?
10. Is increased media volume being mistaken for real-world escalation?
11. Are several unrelated developments being falsely combined?
12. Is geographic spread demonstrated?
13. Is mobilization demonstrated?
14. Is movement from rhetoric to action demonstrated?
15. Are watchlist items actually feasible?

If the evidence does not demonstrate a changed risk mechanism:

prefer:

`STABLE`

or:

`INSUFFICIENT DATA`

rather than forcing:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`
