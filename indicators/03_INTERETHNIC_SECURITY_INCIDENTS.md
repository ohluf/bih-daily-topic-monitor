# EARLY WARNING INDICATORS — INTERETHNIC AND SECURITY INCIDENTS

## Linked topic

`TOP-BIH-INTERETHNIC-SECURITY-INCIDENTS`

## Topic name

Динамика на междуетническите инциденти и инцидентите по сигурността в Босна и Херцеговина

---

# Purpose

This file defines the early-warning and escalation-indicator framework for:

`TOP-BIH-INTERETHNIC-SECURITY-INCIDENTS`

Its purpose is to help the BiH Daily Topic Monitor distinguish between:

- ordinary crime;
- personal conflict;
- public-order incident;
- hate-related incident;
- possible interethnic incident;
- probable interethnic incident;
- confirmed interethnic incident;
- isolated security incident;
- repeated incident pattern;
- geographic spread;
- coordinated provocation;
- broader security deterioration.

The monitor must avoid both:

- overstating ethnic motive;
- missing a genuine escalation pattern.

The central analytical principle is:

**Different ethnic identities of participants do not prove an ethnic motive.**

---

# Exact monitored variable

The monitored variable is:

**Change in the frequency, severity, geographic distribution, target pattern, motive profile, organizational character, repetition, and escalation potential of interethnic incidents and other security incidents capable of affecting community tension and stability in BiH.**

The monitor is not measuring:

- all crime in BiH;
- all public-order violations;
- all interpersonal violence;
- all vandalism;
- all online insults;
- every dispute involving people of different ethnic identities.

The central question is:

**Is there observable change in incident frequency, severity, motive profile, geographic spread, target selection, or pattern formation that materially affects inter-community or broader security risk?**

---

# Core analytical distinction

Always distinguish:

1. INCIDENT OCCURRED
2. INCIDENT TYPE
3. MOTIVE STATUS
4. SEVERITY
5. TARGET
6. ORGANIZATIONAL CHARACTER
7. REPEAT PATTERN
8. GEOGRAPHIC SPREAD
9. OFFICIAL RESPONSE
10. BROADER SECURITY EFFECT

These are not interchangeable.

A serious incident may have:

`MOTIVE UNCONFIRMED`

A low-severity incident may still be analytically important if it forms part of a repeated pattern.

A violent incident between members of different ethnic groups may still be:

`NON-ETHNIC INCIDENT`

A politically amplified incident is not automatically an interethnic incident.

---

# Required motive classification

For every potentially interethnic incident classify:

- CONFIRMED ETHNIC MOTIVE
- PROBABLE ETHNIC MOTIVE
- POSSIBLE ETHNIC MOTIVE
- MOTIVE UNCONFIRMED
- NON-ETHNIC INCIDENT

Do not skip motive classification when the incident is included under this topic.

---

# CONFIRMED ETHNIC MOTIVE

Use only when strong evidence directly supports ethnic, national, religious, or inter-community motive.

Possible evidence includes:

- explicit offender statement;
- verified ethnic slurs directly linked to the act;
- official police classification;
- prosecutorial classification;
- court finding;
- authenticated threatening message;
- target selection clearly based on ethnic or religious identity;
- repeated pattern with direct evidence;
- documented organized hate motivation.

Do not use CONFIRMED merely because:

- victim and offender have different ethnic backgrounds;
- media calls it ethnic;
- a politician alleges ethnic motive;
- the incident occurred in a divided community.

---

# PROBABLE ETHNIC MOTIVE

Use when evidence strongly indicates ethnic or inter-community motive but definitive confirmation is incomplete.

Possible indicators:

- direct ethnic slurs reported by multiple credible witnesses;
- symbolic target strongly connected to community identity;
- timing around a sensitive commemorative event;
- offender behavior directly focused on ethnicity or religion;
- repeated targeting of the same ethnic or religious community;
- prior related incidents involving same actor or network;
- strong contextual and behavioral evidence.

PROBABLE requires more than speculation.

---

# POSSIBLE ETHNIC MOTIVE

Use when an ethnic dimension is plausible but evidence remains incomplete.

Examples:

- contested symbolic target;
- disputed witness accounts;
- ethnic slur alleged but not independently confirmed;
- incident occurs in an already tense local setting;
- motive investigation remains open;
- community representatives raise concern without official confirmation.

Possible motive must not be presented as established fact.

---

# MOTIVE UNCONFIRMED

Use when:

- incident is real;
- participants or target are known;
- ethnic relevance is alleged or plausible;
- evidence does not establish motive.

This is often the correct classification during the first hours of an incident.

Do not rush to upgrade motive.

---

# NON-ETHNIC INCIDENT

Use when evidence indicates:

- personal dispute;
- criminal motive;
- domestic conflict;
- property dispute;
- traffic conflict;
- unrelated hooliganism;
- ordinary crime;
- another clearly established non-ethnic motive.

Do not retain an incident under the interethnic dimension merely because initial media speculation suggested ethnic motive.

---

# Baseline / normal condition

The following must not automatically be treated as interethnic escalation:

- isolated verbal dispute;
- ordinary crime involving different ethnic identities;
- political argument;
- individual insult;
- vandalism without motive evidence;
- personal assault;
- traffic altercation;
- neighborhood dispute;
- football-related disorder without ethnic relevance;
- social-media abuse without real-world consequence;
- routine police action;
- isolated hate speech without escalation mechanism;
- political claims of discrimination without incident evidence;
- repeated media coverage of an old incident.

These may still be relevant as signals.

Additional evidence is required before they can drive escalation assessment.

---

# Indicator interpretation rule

Indicators are classified as:

- LOW-LEVEL
- MEDIUM-LEVEL
- HIGH-LEVEL
- CRITICAL

This classification reflects warning value.

It does not automatically determine:

- DAILY ASSESSMENT;
- SIGNIFICANCE;
- OVERALL DAILY ASSESSMENT.

Always assess:

- incident count;
- distinct incident count;
- severity;
- injuries;
- weapons;
- motive evidence;
- target type;
- repeat pattern;
- geography;
- organization;
- official response;
- retaliation risk.

---

# LOW-LEVEL INDICATORS

Low-level indicators may indicate increased community pressure but do not independently establish escalation.

Examples include:

- isolated ethnic or religious insult;
- graffiti with nationalist content;
- low-level symbolic vandalism;
- hostile social-media post tied to a local dispute;
- isolated provocative flag display;
- verbal confrontation at a sensitive event;
- low-severity damage to a symbolic object;
- isolated threat without demonstrated capability;
- minor provocation around memorial or religious site;
- isolated community complaint;
- unverified allegation of ethnic intimidation;
- politically amplified personal dispute;
- increased hostile rhetoric around a local incident.

## Interpretation

LOW-level indicators may justify:

- local monitoring;
- motive verification;
- police-source search;
- pattern search.

They should not independently justify:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`

---

# MEDIUM-LEVEL INDICATORS

Medium-level indicators demonstrate meaningful movement beyond isolated rhetoric or minor disorder.

Examples include:

- confirmed hate-related assault;
- probable ethnic motive in a physical attack;
- repeated attacks on members of the same community;
- repeated vandalism of religious sites;
- repeated targeting of returnees;
- organized provocation at sensitive event;
- credible threat campaign against a community;
- several distinct incidents in one locality;
- similar incidents in nearby municipalities;
- use of weapons without mass-casualty intent;
- arson attack with possible community motive;
- coordinated intimidation;
- repeated nationalist incidents around same symbolic date;
- significant police deployment due to community tension;
- official warning of increased local risk;
- incident causing meaningful protest or counter-mobilization;
- credible evidence of copycat behavior.

## Interpretation

A verified MEDIUM-level indicator may support:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`

when:

- it represents genuine movement from the previous condition;
- the changed risk mechanism is explicit;
- evidence is sufficiently verified.

Several mutually reinforcing MEDIUM indicators may form an emerging pattern.

---

# HIGH-LEVEL INDICATORS

High-level indicators demonstrate substantial deterioration.

Examples include:

- repeated violent incidents with confirmed or probable ethnic motive;
- attacks in multiple locations;
- coordinated attacks on religious or community sites;
- serious injuries linked to inter-community confrontation;
- firearms used in a politically or ethnically relevant incident;
- organized retaliatory incidents;
- mass public disorder with interethnic dimension;
- repeated attacks on returnee communities;
- organized targeting of symbolic sites;
- severe intimidation causing community displacement or withdrawal;
- credible threats backed by organizational capability;
- rapid geographic spread of similar incidents;
- clear copycat pattern;
- evidence that political actors are mobilizing around incidents;
- direct involvement of organized radical networks;
- official extraordinary security measures;
- credible warning from security institutions of broader risk.

## Interpretation

HIGH-level indicators may justify:

`ESCALATING`

when:

- verified;
- materially new;
- consequential;
- linked to a clear worsening mechanism.

---

# CRITICAL INDICATORS

Critical indicators represent acute or immediate risk.

Examples include:

- large-scale interethnic violence;
- coordinated armed attacks;
- mass-casualty incident with ethnic or community motive;
- rapid retaliatory violence;
- multiple simultaneous attacks;
- organized armed mobilization around community lines;
- attacks on civilians based on identity;
- direct clashes between organized community groups;
- widespread destruction of religious or community sites;
- confirmed operational planning for mass violence;
- forced displacement due to organized threats;
- breakdown of local security control;
- emergency national or international security measures linked to imminent violence.

## Interpretation

CRITICAL requires:

- strongest available verification;
- active contradiction search;
- careful distinction between allegation and confirmed event.

CRITICAL must remain exceptional.

---

# Incident severity ladder

Assess incidents on the following ladder:

VERBAL / SYMBOLIC
→ THREAT
→ PROPERTY DAMAGE
→ PHYSICAL ASSAULT
→ SERIOUS INJURY
→ WEAPON USE
→ ORGANIZED VIOLENCE
→ MULTI-LOCATION VIOLENCE
→ MASS-CASUALTY EVENT

Movement upward on this ladder may indicate escalation.

But severity alone is not sufficient.

Also assess:

- motive;
- repetition;
- organization;
- geographic spread.

---

# Pattern ladder

Assess whether evidence reflects:

ISOLATED INCIDENT
→ REPEATED SIMILAR INCIDENTS
→ LOCAL CLUSTER
→ MULTI-LOCATION CLUSTER
→ EMERGING PATTERN
→ COORDINATED PATTERN

Do not jump from:

two incidents

to:

coordinated pattern

without evidence of linkage.

---

# Escalation mechanisms

The monitor must identify the exact mechanism.

Use:

PREVIOUS CONDITION
→ NEW DEVELOPMENT
→ CHANGED RISK

---

## Mechanism 1 — Isolated incident to repetition

Single incident
→ additional similar incidents
→ increased probability of pattern formation

---

## Mechanism 2 — Low-severity to higher severity

Verbal or symbolic incidents
→ physical attacks or weapon use
→ increased security risk

---

## Mechanism 3 — Local to geographic spread

One municipality
→ similar incidents in additional locations
→ broader inter-community risk

---

## Mechanism 4 — Incident to retaliation

Initial incident
→ retaliatory act
→ self-reinforcing escalation cycle

---

## Mechanism 5 — Individual to organized activity

Personal incident
→ coordinated group involvement
→ higher capability and repeat risk

---

## Mechanism 6 — Online to offline

Threatening narrative
→ physical mobilization or attack
→ increased real-world risk

---

## Mechanism 7 — Symbolic target to community confrontation

Attack on religious or memorial site
→ mobilized community reaction
→ increased probability of reciprocal incidents

---

## Mechanism 8 — Political amplification to real-world escalation

Incident
→ inflammatory political amplification
→ protest, mobilization, or retaliation

---

## Mechanism 9 — Local incident to cross-border reinforcement

Local event
→ external network or actor amplification
→ increased persistence or escalation potential

If no clear mechanism exists:

do not force escalation.

---

# Sequence indicators

Potential meaningful sequences include:

Graffiti
→ repeated vandalism
→ attack on religious site
→ protest
→ retaliatory incident

or:

Online threat
→ mobilization call
→ gathering
→ physical confrontation

or:

Attack on returnee
→ political amplification
→ copycat incident
→ geographic spread

or:

Symbolic provocation
→ counter-protest
→ police deployment
→ clashes

or:

Local incident
→ organized external support
→ broader mobilization

A sequence is stronger evidence than unrelated incidents.

# Primary evidence requirement

Assessment must be driven mainly by:

`PRIMARY ASSESSMENT EVIDENCE`

Examples:

- verified distinct incident;
- official police report;
- prosecutorial information;
- confirmed injury;
- verified weapon use;
- verified repeat incident;
- documented target pattern;
- confirmed geographic spread;
- reliable motive evidence;
- organized participation.

The following normally remain:

`SUPPORTING CONTEXT`

unless they materially change risk:

- media commentary;
- political statements;
- anniversary sensitivity;
- historical grievances;
- repeated reporting;
- community concern without incident evidence;
- social-media outrage.

---

# Incident count discipline

Always distinguish:

- ARTICLE COUNT
- CLAIM COUNT
- VERIFIED INCIDENT COUNT
- DISTINCT INCIDENT COUNT

Example:

15 articles about one attack
=
1 underlying incident

Do not report media volume as incident growth.

---

# Duplicate incident discipline

Two reports may describe:

- the same incident;
- the same victim;
- the same location;
- the same police case.

Check:

- date;
- time;
- location;
- victim;
- offender;
- police case;
- source chain.

Do not count duplicates separately.

---

# Incident identity discipline

For every included incident identify, where possible:

- DATE
- TIME
- LOCATION
- MUNICIPALITY
- ENTITY / CANTON
- INCIDENT TYPE
- TARGET
- VICTIM STATUS
- OFFENDER STATUS
- INJURIES
- WEAPONS
- MOTIVE STATUS
- POLICE STATUS
- PROSECUTOR STATUS
- SOURCE CONFIDENCE

This internal structure does not need to be published in full.

---

# Local authority discipline

For each significant local incident identify the competent authority.

Possible sources:

- local police station;
- police administration;
- cantonal MUP;
- MUP RS;
- FUP;
- prosecutor;
- municipality.

National media should not replace local competent-authority checks when such checks are possible.

---

# Motive evidence hierarchy

Stronger motive evidence includes:

1. Court finding
2. Prosecutorial classification
3. Police classification
4. Authenticated offender statement
5. Verified direct threat
6. Multiple credible eyewitness accounts
7. Clear symbolic target pattern
8. Contextual inference
9. Political allegation
10. Media speculation

Do not treat lower-level evidence as equivalent to higher-level confirmation.

---

# Hate-speech discipline

A hate statement may be relevant.

But distinguish:

HATE SPEECH
from
THREAT

and:

THREAT
from
OPERATIONAL CAPABILITY

A hostile post does not automatically mean imminent violence.

---

# Religious-site incident discipline

For incidents involving:

- mosque;
- church;
- cemetery;
- memorial;
- religious symbol;

assess:

- exact damage;
- motive evidence;
- repeat history;
- timing;
- offender identity;
- official response;
- community reaction.

Do not automatically assume ethnic motive.

But treat repeated targeting as analytically important.

---

# Returnee incident discipline

Incidents involving returnees may carry elevated sensitivity.

Assess:

- whether victim status is relevant to motive;
- whether same community is repeatedly targeted;
- whether authorities acknowledge a pattern;
- whether displacement or fear effects appear.

Do not assume motive solely from returnee status.

---

# Symbolic-date discipline

Sensitive dates may increase contextual relevance.

Examples may include:

- commemorations;
- religious holidays;
- war anniversaries;
- memorial events.

But:

sensitive date
→ not automatic escalation

Look for:

- actual incidents;
- mobilization;
- threats;
- police posture change;
- repeated targeting.

---

# Weapons discipline

Distinguish:

- weapon present;
- weapon displayed;
- weapon threatened;
- weapon fired;
- weapon caused injury;
- organized weapons use.

A firearm in an unrelated criminal incident does not automatically create interethnic relevance.

---

# Injury discipline

Distinguish:

- no injury;
- minor injury;
- serious injury;
- life-threatening injury;
- fatality;
- multiple casualties.

Do not use vague wording when reliable information exists.

---

# Official response discipline

Possible response levels include:

- no official response;
- preliminary police check;
- investigation;
- arrest;
- prosecutorial review;
- charge;
- indictment;
- court proceeding;
- conviction.

Do not upgrade one into another.

---

# De-escalation indicators

Possible de-escalation indicators include:

- no follow-on incidents after a cluster;
- successful police intervention;
- credible clarification of non-ethnic motive;
- withdrawal of retaliatory calls;
- cancellation of confrontational gatherings;
- community mediation;
- joint condemnation by relevant actors;
- arrests disrupting organized activity;
- reduced incident frequency;
- restoration of local confidence;
- reopening of affected institutions or sites;
- absence of geographic spread after initial concern.

A quiet day alone does not prove de-escalation.

---

# Stabilization indicators

Possible stabilization indicators include:

- isolated incident remains isolated;
- no retaliation;
- no copycat events;
- motive remains non-ethnic;
- no geographic spread;
- authorities contain the incident;
- political amplification does not produce mobilization;
- community reactions remain peaceful.

These may support:

`STABLE`

# Evidence requirements

For LOW-level incidents:

- credible local reporting may be sufficient for discovery;
- official check preferred.

For MEDIUM-level incidents:

- seek police confirmation;
- prosecutor information;
- or credible independent corroboration.

For HIGH-level incidents:

- seek official evidence;
- multiple genuinely independent sources;
- active motive verification;
- contradiction search.

For CRITICAL incidents:

- use highest available verification standard;
- actively test:
  - casualty numbers;
  - location;
  - timing;
  - motive;
  - organization;
  - old footage;
  - manipulated media.

---

# Source hierarchy for this topic

Prioritize where relevant:

1. Local police
2. Cantonal or entity police
3. Prosecutors
4. Courts
5. Local authorities
6. Direct victim or witness material with verification
7. Established local media
8. Established national BiH media
9. Independent corroboration
10. Community representatives
11. Social media as signal

Do not use hierarchy mechanically.

Source role matters.

# Daily assessment decision rules

## STABLE

Use when:

- incidents remain isolated;
- no verified increase in severity or frequency appears;
- no pattern or spread is demonstrated.

---

## LIMITED ESCALATION

Use when:

- at least one verified development materially increases risk;
- there is a clear mechanism such as:
  - repeat incident;
  - increased severity;
  - retaliation;
  - geographic spread;
  - organized participation;
- scope remains limited.

Before assigning, state internally:

PREVIOUS CONDITION
→ NEW DEVELOPMENT
→ CHANGED RISK

---

## ESCALATING

Use when:

- several primary indicators worsen;
- incident frequency increases;
- severity increases;
- geographic spread appears;
- retaliation develops;
- organized participation strengthens.

---

## MIXED

Use only when:

- one dimension worsens;
- another genuinely improves.

Do not use MIXED merely because some incidents are confirmed and others unconfirmed.

---

## INSUFFICIENT DATA

Use when:

- incident identity is unclear;
- duplicates cannot be excluded;
- motive remains speculative;
- source quality is weak;
- trend cannot be distinguished from media noise.

---

# Significance discipline

Do not confuse:

`SIGNIFICANCE`

with:

`ESCALATION`

or:

`CONFIDENCE`

Example:

One severe isolated attack may be:

HIGH significance
+
STABLE broader pattern

Example:

Several lower-severity repeated incidents may be:

MEDIUM significance individually
+
LIMITED ESCALATION collectively

---

# Watchlist requirements

Watch indicators must be:

- specific;
- observable;
- procedurally possible;
- temporally plausible.

Good examples:

- whether police classify motive;
- whether a second similar incident appears in same municipality;
- whether prosecutor opens a formal case;
- whether retaliatory calls produce physical gatherings;
- whether similar attacks spread to another locality;
- whether the same target type is repeated;
- whether initial casualty figures are revised;
- whether independent evidence confirms alleged ethnic slurs.

Bad examples:

- “monitor ethnic tensions”;
- “watch for more incidents”;
- “see whether situation worsens”.

---

# Final topic audit

Before assigning the daily assessment for:

`TOP-BIH-INTERETHNIC-SECURITY-INCIDENTS`

verify:

1. How many distinct incidents actually occurred?
2. Are duplicate reports removed?
3. What is the exact incident type?
4. What is the motive status?
5. Is ethnic motive confirmed, probable, possible, unconfirmed, or absent?
6. What is the severity?
7. Were weapons used?
8. Were there injuries?
9. Is the target pattern meaningful?
10. Is the incident isolated or repeated?
11. Is there geographic spread?
12. Is retaliation present?
13. Is organized participation demonstrated?
14. Are local competent authorities checked?
15. Are political claims being mistaken for evidence?
16. Is media volume being mistaken for incident growth?
17. Is the escalation mechanism explicit?
18. Would the assessment remain justified if unconfirmed motive claims were removed?
19. Are source chains genuinely independent?
20. Are watchlist items concrete and feasible?

If the evidence does not demonstrate a changed incident-risk mechanism:

prefer:

`STABLE`

or:

`INSUFFICIENT DATA`

rather than forcing:

`LIMITED ESCALATION`
